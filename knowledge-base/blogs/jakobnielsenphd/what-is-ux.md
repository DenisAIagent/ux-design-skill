# What Is UX?

**Date:** 2023-08-14

**Source:** [Original Article](https://jakobnielsenphd.substack.com/p/what-is-ux)

---

## Summary

Jakob Nielsen explains that user experience represents "we make computers easy to use," though the concept encompasses much more. The article traces UX origins to Bell Labs in the 1950s and explores how this discipline has grown to involve approximately 3 million professionals worldwide.

## The Genesis of UX

While Don Norman coined the term "user experience" in 1993, the discipline's roots extend back to 1945 when Bell Labs hired John E. Karlin. The organization developed a human-centered approach to designing telephony interfaces rather than purely engineering-driven projects.

The touchstone telephone keypad design exemplifies this methodology. Bell Labs researchers tested multiple layouts with actual users, timing entries and recording errors. The company selected the design performing best in empirical testing--the same layout still used today on mobile phones.

This single decision proved remarkably valuable. Nielsen estimates the interface has been used approximately 40 trillion times, saving "a collective one million person-years of time" compared to reasonable alternative designs.

## The Duality of UX: Process and Result

User experience encompasses both process and outcome. As a result, "UX simplifies technology" across diverse applications--from hospital navigation to tax form completion, hotel check-ins, and train journey improvements.

## Defining UX vs. UI

The core distinction separates user-centered design from engineering-centered approaches. Researchers must study actual human behavior through empirical investigation rather than assumptions.

"User Experience" represents the complete customer interaction across all touchpoints. However, designers actually shape components creating this experience--most importantly, the user interface itself. The UI encompasses on-screen elements, interaction design, information architecture, and content presentation.

## Usability: UX's Quality Compass

Usability comprises five components:

- **Learnability**: New user adoption speed
- **Efficiency**: Productivity once learned
- **Memorability**: Performance after breaks
- **Errors**: Mistake frequency, severity, and recovery
- **Satisfaction**: User pleasure from interaction

The relative importance of these factors varies by product type. Single-use systems require excellent learnability, while enterprise software prioritizes efficiency.

## Conclusion

UX represents a scientific methodology applied to design, emphasizing empathy and observation. By transforming technology from engineering-focused to human-centered, organizations improve both user satisfaction and profitability.
