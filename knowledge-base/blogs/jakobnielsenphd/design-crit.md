# How to Run a UX Design Critique

**Date:** 2026-03-12

**Source:** [Original Article](https://jakobnielsenphd.substack.com/p/design-crit)

---

## Summary

Jakob Nielsen presents a comprehensive guide to conducting effective design critiques, emphasizing that success requires "strategic participant roles, 24-hour context share, and rigorous prioritization frameworks." This approach helps cross-functional teams align technical feasibility with business objectives while mitigating development risks.

## How Design Crits Go Wrong

Nielsen identifies thirteen common pitfalls that derail design critiques:

1. **Context Vacuum** - Presenting designs without explaining user personas, problems, or constraints
2. **Solutionizing** - Reviewers prescribe specific fixes rather than identifying underlying problems
3. **Bikeshedding** - Debating minor details at inappropriate fidelity levels
4. **Subjective Feedback** - Comments based on personal taste rather than user needs or evidence
5. **Designer Defensiveness** - Presenter argues against feedback instead of listening
6. **Status Rituals** - Feedback weighted by organizational rank rather than relevance
7. **Vague Comments** - Unactionable phrases like "make it pop"
8. **Presenter Defensiveness** - Designer takes criticism personally
9. **HiPPO Effect** - Highest-paid person's opinion dominates discussion
10. **Unfacilitated Chaos** - Discussions derail into tangential topics
11. **Design by Committee** - Attempting to incorporate all feedback equally
12. **Black Hole** - Failing to document decisions and follow-up
13. **Late Critique** - Feedback delivered after substantial investment

Nielsen notes that beneath these failures lies a fundamental shift: "critique stops being a method for examining design decisions and becomes a social event organized around opinion, hierarchy, and momentum."

## Structural Planning and Foundations

### Participant Selection

Optimal critique groups include 3-8 participants, allowing diverse perspectives without overwhelming facilitation. Cross-functional representation from Engineering and Product Management ensures feedback considers technical feasibility and business viability.

### The 24-Hour Context Share

Distributing materials one day before the meeting transforms feedback quality. Pre-review allows critics to move beyond surface reactions toward structural analysis anchored in "user needs, business objectives, and technical feasibility."

### Framing the Presentation

Effective presentations require:

- **Scope and Maturity Declaration** - Explicitly state whether design is exploratory ("mushy") or nearly final ("baked")
- **Narrative Journeys** - Show user flows across screens rather than isolated moments
- **Design Rationale** - Connect visual choices to research, business goals, or constraints

## Facilitation and Flow

### Ground Rules

Before sessions begin, establish:
- Focus on work, not person
- Provide specific, actionable feedback
- Reference goals and users
- Suggest options, not demands

### Session Structure (45-minute example)

- Opening/Context (5 min)
- Silent Feedback (10 min)
- Verbal Discussion (20 min)
- Synthesis (10 min)

The silent feedback phase proves critical, allowing introverted participants equal voice and filtering initial reactions into deliberate analysis.

## Psychological Safety

Design requires "an art of being wrong safely." Teams foster safety through:

- **Linguistic Distancing** - Refer to "the design" rather than "your design"
- **Leadership Modeling** - Senior designers share unfinished work openly
- **Normalized Iteration** - Acknowledge constraints and trade-offs

## Synthesis and Prioritization

### Affinity Mapping

Organize raw feedback into themed clusters through:
1. Atomizing each observation
2. Grouping by natural connection
3. Labeling thematically
4. Recognizing systemic patterns
5. Visualizing relationships

### Feedback Format: I Like, I Wish, What If

This structure balances affirmation with constructive improvement:
- **I Like** - Reinforces successful patterns
- **I Wish** - Frames improvements constructively
- **What If** - Explores possibilities without demanding implementation

### Prioritization Frameworks

The Impact/Effort matrix plots change value against implementation complexity. The RICE framework calculates scores based on Reach, Impact, Confidence, and Effort, removing emotional bias from decision-making.

Severity categorization helps teams address critical issues first:
- Blocker (task completion impossible)
- Major Issue (significant struggle)
- Minor Issue (completion with frustration)
- Cosmetic (aesthetic only)

## Real-World Examples

### Airbnb
Emphasizes tangibility through physical walls and interactive prototypes, prioritizing "journeys" via storyboards to identify experience gaps.

### IBM
Uses "Playbacks" and "Hills" (user-focused outcome statements) to maintain alignment across dispersed teams.

### Spotify
Balances exploration with structure through a "Sandbox" model, using asynchronous video walkthroughs for remote teams.

### Google
Employs rigorous "Understand" phases with Lightning Talks on research, goals, and feasibility before prototype and rapid testing cycles.

## AI-Enhanced Critiques

Modern AI can strengthen every critique stage:

**Preparation:** Generate tailored agendas, convert designs into hypotheses, conduct pre-critique analysis, synthesize asynchronous feedback.

**During Sessions:** Analyze discussions for confusion signals, counter groupthink, de-escalate conflicts, act as synthetic stakeholders.

**Follow-Through:** Synthesize feedback into summaries, extract action items, cluster themes, convert to prioritized tickets, perform longitudinal analysis.

Nielsen emphasizes that "AI's most valuable contribution is not producing more feedback, but helping teams ask the right questions at the right abstraction level."

## Key Takeaway

Successful design critiques shift from subjective opinion-based discussions to structured, evidence-driven conversations anchored in user needs and business objectives. This requires strategic participant selection, psychological safety, clear facilitation, rigorous synthesis, and thoughtful follow-up.
