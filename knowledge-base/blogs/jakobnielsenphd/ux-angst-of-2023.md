# UX Angst of 2023-24

**Date:** 2023-09-13

**Source:** [Original Article](https://jakobnielsenphd.substack.com/p/ux-angst-of-2023)

---

## Summary

Jakob Nielsen addresses widespread pessimism in the UX field during 2023-24, arguing that concerns about UX's decline are overblown. While acknowledging real challenges like salary decreases and job cuts, he reframes these as market corrections rather than catastrophic failures.

## Bad News of 2023

The UX industry faced genuine headwinds:

- UX salaries declined 11% compared to 2022
- UX researcher job postings dropped 73% year-over-year on Indeed.com
- UX designer positions fell 71% during the same period
- Tech companies laid off thousands of UX professionals

However, Nielsen contextualizes these statistics. The 2022-2023 drops represent a "market correction" following an unsustainable hiring bubble from 2020-2022. When compared to 2018 levels, UX research positions remained "53% higher," indicating longer-term growth despite recent declines.

## Good News of 2024

Nielsen identifies AI as the driving force for UX's future renaissance. The emergence of AI systems creates demand for substantial UX research and design work focused on improving user interfaces around artificial intelligence.

He applies "Saffo's Law": "Never mistake a clear view for a short distance." While the AI-driven opportunity appears clear, the timing remains uncertain.

## Is UX Disintegrating?

The author directly challenges the "secular decline" narrative. The UX profession has grown exponentially--from approximately 1,000 professionals globally 40 years ago to nearly 3 million today. This 3,000-fold expansion means most companies employing UX staff remain early-stage practitioners, which naturally produces lower maturity levels.

Nielsen notes that "most practitioners are green" as an inevitable mathematical consequence of exponential growth, not evidence of declining standards. UX maturity has actually "improved every decade."

## Professional Context

Nielsen draws on 41 years of UX experience, including periods through the dot-com crash (2001) and the Great Recession (2009)--both far more severe than current conditions. He challenges the notion that earlier eras represented some "golden age" of UX practice.

The message: embrace the field's exponential growth as success. Today's newcomers will become tomorrow's senior practitioners in a profession projected to reach 10 million practitioners by 2035.
