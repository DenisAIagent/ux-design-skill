# Don't Design for Average Users

**Date:** March 19, 2026
**Source:** https://jakobnielsenphd.substack.com/p/p50-vs-p95

Jakob Nielsen argues that designing for the "average user" is fundamentally flawed. Instead, product teams should recognize and design for the radical disparity between median (P50) and 95th-percentile (P95) users.

## The P95/P50 Ratio Problem

Digital engagement follows power-law distributions rather than normal bell curves. The arithmetic mean creates a "mathematical ghost" — a phantom user that doesn't actually exist.

- **E-Commerce:** 3-10x ratio (bounded by physical constraints)
- **Enterprise SaaS:** 6-17x ratio (specialized workflows expand gaps)
- **Social Media:** 30-550x ratio (discretionary use creates extremes)
- **Mobile Gaming:** Effectively infinite ratio (median spend = $0)

## Two User Archetypes

**The P50 "Tourist"** exhibits passive consumption, high friction aversion, and minimal economic contribution. They adopt only 16% of available features.

**The P95 "Whale"** drives disproportionate revenue, content creation, and product learning. They adopt 45%+ of features and generate the ecosystem's economic engine.

## Design Strategy: "Tale of Two Cities"

Progressive disclosure — a simple on-ramp for casual users with excavated depths for power users:

- Command palettes (Cmd+K) for keyboard shortcuts
- API access and automation for advanced workflows
- Separate admin dashboards from primary interfaces
- "Fattening the tail" by converting P50 users into P95 users

## Practical Implications

Track P25, P50, P75, and P95 metrics instead of means. Recognize that losing one P95 user often equals losing dozens of median users. Price according to usage skew — flat rates work for mild ratios (2-5x); variable pricing suits steep ones (20x+).

"The Whales aren't an edge case: they pay the bills."
