# The Capability Maturity Model for AI in Design

**Date:** 2026-03-05

**Source:** [Original Article](https://jakobnielsenphd.substack.com/p/ai-maturity)

---

## Summary

Jakob Nielsen presents a six-level capability maturity model for AI integration in design work, extending Matt Davey's original five-level framework. The model evaluates organizations across leadership, strategy, culture, enablement, automation, and product design dimensions.

## The Six Maturity Levels

**Level 1 (Limited):** AI serves only administrative tasks. Leadership views it skeptically, with no organizational strategy or budget allocation. Design work remains fully manual, and AI in products appears as superficial additions.

**Level 2 (Reactive):** Ad-hoc experimentation dominates, driven by pressure to "ship AI" rather than solve user problems. Teams lack structured learning, producing inconsistent results and throwaway work without maintenance frameworks.

**Level 3 (Developing):** AI becomes a recognized prototyping accelerator with problem-led adoption. Teams invest in specific tools and documented playbooks, enabling reproducible patterns and peer learning.

**Level 4 (Embedded):** AI integrates into core design infrastructure with deep alignment between design and engineering. High-fidelity prototypes using real components reduce handoff friction.

**Level 5 (Leading):** Designers work natively in code through "vibe coding," with responsibility shifting from artifact creation to strategic oversight. Responsible AI becomes synonymous with product quality.

**Level 6 (Symbiotic):** The distinction between design-time and runtime dissolves. "AI agents dynamically generate and optimize user interfaces in real-time based on individual user context," with designers functioning as ecosystem gardeners rather than pixel-pushers.

## Critical Challenges

The article identifies three major shifts required for maturity progression:

1. **From Deterministic to Probabilistic Design:** Traditional UI operates predictably; AI-driven experiences require planning for failure modes and uncertainty management.

2. **From Creation to Curation:** Designers transition from generating options to establishing evaluation criteria and safety boundaries that guide autonomous AI outputs.

3. **The Junior Designer Dilemma:** As automation eliminates foundational pixel-pushing work, organizations must create new pathways for developing junior talent's taste and judgment.

## Sequential Progression Imperative

Nielsen emphasizes that maturity levels cannot be skipped. Each level builds interdependent capabilities across six characteristics; attempting to leapfrog creates "capability debt" leading to system failure. Organizations can realistically advance two levels annually with focused effort, making the jump from Level 1 to Level 5 approximately two years of sustained improvement.

Most organizations should target Level 5 by 2026, with Level 6 remaining achievable only by elite AI-native startups capable of operating autonomous systems reliably.
