# Dark Design Patterns Catalog

**Date:** 2023-12-06

**Source:** [Original Article](https://jakobnielsenphd.substack.com/p/dark-design)

---

## Overview

Jakob Nielsen examines thirteen dark design patterns that the Government of India recently prohibited. These unethical UX practices manipulate users through misleading interfaces or unnecessary friction for actions companies want to prevent--like subscription cancellations.

## The Patterns

**False Urgency** (5/5 skulls) -- Falsely claiming limited availability or popularity to rush purchase decisions. This exploits the scarcity principle from behavioral economics through active deception.

**Basket Sneaking** (5/5 skulls) -- Adding unrequested items, services, or charitable donations at checkout without consent. Essentially stealing from inattentive users despite items appearing as line items.

**Confirmshaming** (1/5 skulls) -- Using guilt, fear, or ridicule to nudge users toward purchases. An example: displaying "I will stay unsecured" when declining travel insurance. Less harmful than other patterns since it doesn't deceive.

**Forced Action** (0-4/5 skulls) -- Preventing users from purchasing intended items without additional purchases or sign-ups. Clear upfront bundling requires no dark rating; hidden requirements rate 4 skulls.

**Subscription Trap** (3/5 skulls) -- Making cancellation significantly harder than signup. Common but substantially inconvenient for users seeking to exit.

**Interface Interference** (2/5 skulls) -- Highlighting certain information while obscuring relevant details. Information remains visible but deliberately de-emphasized through smaller fonts or poor placement.

**Bait and Switch** (4/5 skulls) -- Advertising one outcome but delivering another. Example: promoting cheap products that become unavailable, with expensive alternatives offered instead.

**Drip Pricing** (2-5/5 skulls) -- Hiding full costs until late in purchase. Revealing extra fees mid-process rates 2 skulls; advertising free services requiring later payment rates 3 skulls; charging more than disclosed at checkout rates 5 skulls.

**Disguised Advertisement** (2/5 skulls) -- Cloaking ads as user-generated content or news articles. Annoying but less harmful than deceptive financial practices.

**Nagging** (1/5 skulls) -- Repeatedly disrupting interactions with persistent requests, hoping users click without reading. Exploits "fly-swatting behavior."

**Trick Question** (3/5 skulls) -- Employing confusing wording, double negatives, or vague language to misdirect users. Nielsen finds this particularly offensive as deliberate obfuscation.

**Billing Surprise** (5/5 skulls) -- Silently converting free trials to paid or debiting accounts without notification. Nearly equivalent to theft through surreptitious charges.

**Rogue Malware** (5/5 skulls) -- Using scareware to convince users their computers have viruses, then selling fake removal tools that install actual malware. Characterized as criminal activity rather than mere design pattern.

## Defense Mechanisms

Nielsen identifies three approaches to combating dark patterns:

1. **Professional pushback** -- UX professionals refusing unethical requests by explaining long-term brand damage
2. **Public awareness** -- Exposing harmful practices through documentation and discussion
3. **Regulation** -- Legal consequences like fines, as enacted in India
