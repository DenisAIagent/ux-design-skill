# Use the AI Transition Period to Transition Your Career

**Date:** 2025-06-19

**Source:** [Original Article](https://jakobnielsenphd.substack.com/p/ai-transition-career-transition)

---

## Summary

Jakob Nielsen argues that UX professionals have approximately five years to reinvent themselves for an AI-driven future. Rather than optimizing legacy UX methods, he encourages practitioners to actively shape emerging AI-native workflows by developing three core human competencies: agency, judgment, and persuasion.

## Three Eras of Computing

Nielsen divides technological history into three periods: the pre-AI era (1945-2022), the current transition phase (2023-2030), and the superintelligence era (beginning around 2030). The present window offers a critical opportunity to prepare before fundamental disruption arrives.

## Abandon Legacy Skills

Nielsen stresses that "the best way to predict the future is to invent it," citing Alan Kay's 1971 insight. He argues that perfecting traditional UX methods resembles becoming an expert mammoth hunter as agriculture emerges--skills that become obsolete regardless of proficiency.

He cautions that "any time you spend on legacy UX is time you don't spend reinventing yourself for the AI future." Instead of incrementally improving current workflows, professionals should experiment with entirely new AI-enabled approaches.

## Three Essential Skills for Tomorrow

**Agency:** Taking initiative to shape work direction rather than waiting for instruction, since AI will execute directives better than humans.

**Judgment:** Making decisions about which AI-generated options to implement, as ideation becomes nearly costless.

**Persuasion:** Influencing stakeholders remains essential since work operates within human communities regardless of AI capabilities.

## Strategic Career Moves

Nielsen recommends pursuing roles at AI-native companies (built from inception around AI), AI-first organizations (legacy companies pivoting toward AI), or AI-forward teams where management supports experimentation. He suggests accepting lower compensation for rapid skill development in emerging domains.
